Page 1 — Authentication, Privacy, Warnings & Frameworks

Auth Core Concepts

  • Authentication = verifying who you are (identity proof)
  • Authorization = what you're allowed to do (permissions)
  • Identification = claiming an identity (e.g. username)

Three Factors

  • Know — password, PIN, secret question
  • Have — phone, YubiKey, smart card
  • Are — fingerprint, face, iris, voice, gait

MFA = 2+ different factors. Two passwords ≠ MFA.

Bonneau et al. — Evaluating Auth

"Quest to Replace Passwords" (IEEE S&P 2012). Checklist to score any auth scheme.

Usability

  • Memory-effortless — no memorization (pw: ✗)
  • Scalable — works for 100s of accounts (pw: ✗)
  • Nothing-to-carry — no physical object (pw: ✓)
  • Physically-effortless — no demanding action (pw: ~✓)
  • Easy-to-learn / Efficient-to-use (pw: ✓)
  • Infrequent-errors (pw: moderate)
  • Easy-recovery — regain access if locked out (pw: ✓)

Deployability

  • Accessible, Negligible-cost, Server-compatible, Browser-compatible, Mature, Non-proprietary (pw: all ✓)

Security

  • Resilient-to-physical-observation — shoulder surfing (pw: ✗)
  • Resilient-to-targeted-impersonation — personal info guessing (pw: ✗)
  • Resilient-to-throttled-guessing — online brute force (pw: partial)
  • Resilient-to-unthrottled-guessing — offline brute force (pw: ✗)
  • Resilient-to-internal-observation — keyloggers (pw: ✗)
  • Resilient-to-leaks — server breach (pw: partial)
  • Resilient-to-phishing — fake sites (pw: ✗)
  • Resilient-to-theft — stealing physical token (pw: N/A)
  • No-trusted-third-party (pw: ✓)
  • Unlinkable — can't correlate across services (pw: ✗ if reused)

Passwords & Biometrics

Passwords

  • "Don't care" region: either so weak they fall to online guessing or so strong offline is infeasible; only middle range benefits from complexity
  • NCSC: 3 random words > complex char rules
  • Password managers: solve scalability+memorability; barrier=trust, usability
  • Strength meters (Ur et al.): stringent meters ↑ strength; text+bar most effective

Solove's Taxonomy of Privacy Harms

1. Collection

  • Surveillance — watching/recording activities
  • Interrogation — compelling info disclosure

2. Processing

  • Aggregation — combining data reveals new info
  • Identification — linking data to a person
  • Insecurity — failing to protect stored data
  • Secondary use — using data for different purpose than collected
  • Exclusion — not letting subject know/control their data

3. Dissemination

  • Breach of confidentiality — breaking secrecy promise
  • Disclosure — revealing truthful info causing harm

AI extensions (Lee): phrenology/physiognomy, new-scale aggregation & identification, exacerbated secondary use & exclusion.

NEAT & SPRUCE Frameworks

NEAT (design good warnings)

  • Necessary — can you eliminate the decision?
  • Explained — plain language, all info needed
  • Actionable — clear steps/buttons for each option
  • Tested — on real users outside your team

SPRUCE (warning content)

  • Source — who shows this? · Process — steps to decide
  • Risk — what bad thing could happen · Unique — user's unique knowledge
  • Choices — options + recommendation · Evidence — supporting info

Herley's Security Advice (2009)

Users rationally ignore advice: cost falls on them, benefit on someone else.

  • Externality — cost/benefit falls on others (bank absorbs fraud)
  • Internality — cost/benefit falls on user
  • Analyze: Cost to user vs Potential benefit vs Actual benefit. If cost high but actual benefit low (externality) → advice not worth following.

Between vs Within Subjects

BetweenWithin
SetupEach participant sees 1 conditionEach sees all conditions
ProNo learning/fatigue effectsLess noise, fewer participants needed
ConIndividual differences, more participantsLearning effects, fatigue; mitigate with counterbalancing

Variables & Causation

  • IV (independent) = what you manipulate · DV (dependent) = what you measure
  • Confounding var = unmeasured factor influencing DV
  • Correlation ≠ causation. Causation needs controlled experiments ruling out confounders.

Lab Study Recipe

  1. RQ: "Does [IV] affect [DV] for [population]?"
  2. Method: Lab/field/survey/think-aloud + justification
  3. Participants: Who, how many (qual ~10-15; quant ~30+/condition), recruitment
  4. Setup: Equipment, environment, prototype
  5. Tasks: Specific, realistic actions
  6. Metrics/DV: Completion rate, time, errors, SUS, Likert
  7. Limitations: Ecological validity, sample bias, small N, social desirability

Qualitative Methods

  • Think-aloud: User verbalizes thoughts during tasks. Cheap, 5 users find major issues. Limitation: changes behavior.
  • Cognitive walkthrough: Expert steps through UI as user ("would user know what to do?")
  • Heuristic eval: Experts rate interface against usability principles
  • Coding: Inductive (bottom-up, thematic analysis) vs Deductive (top-down, pre-existing framework)
  • IRR (inter-rater reliability): Cohen's κ, >0.6 acceptable · Intra-rater: same coder consistency over time

Dark Patterns

UI tricks making users do unintended things or making opt-out unreasonably hard.

Cookie Banner Dark Patterns

  • Pre-ticked boxes · Hidden reject (3 sub-menus) · Confusing language · Visual asymmetry (big Accept, tiny Decline) · Forced action (blocked content)

Harms: erodes consent, financial harm, undermines trust, harms vulnerable users disproportionately.

Informed Consent & Privacy Policies

Informed consent: Person understands what they agree to, what data collected, how used, risks, can withdraw — before agreeing. Burying in T&C ≠ informed.

3 Policy Formats

  • Full policy — comprehensive, nobody reads
  • Nutrition label — table summary, easy to scan
  • Machine-readable — XML/JSON for auto-checking

FTC: Fines for violating own policy (Google Buzz, Snapchat). Trust barriers (Riegelsberger & Sasse): lack of knowledge, experience, separation in space/time. Trust transfer: positive experience → trusts similar-looking sites.

Phishing & Fraud

Ecosystem: Phisher → Email server (filters) → User (clicks or reports) → Help desk (blocks URL, updates filters).

4 Countermeasures

  • 1. Automated blocking — spam filters, URL blacklists, DMARC
  • 2. User training — Anti-Phishing Phil, embedded training, NoPhish; fades over time
  • 3. UI support — lock icon, domain highlighting, warnings; users ignore them
  • 4. Credential protection — 2FA, pw managers, WebAuthn/FIDO2

Stanford Fraud Taxonomy (7)

Access Control

DACOwner decides access by identity; can re-share
MACSystem-enforced labels/clearance. Bell-LaPadula: no read up, no write down
RBACPermissions → roles → users. Scalable.
  • ACL = per-object ("who can access this?") · Capability = per-subject ("what can this user access?")
  • Least privilege = minimum permissions needed

Smart Home (Zeng & Roesner): preferences differ per capability; relationships matter most; restrictive by default.

AI Security & Privacy

Attacks

  • Data poisoning — malicious training examples
  • Inversion — reconstruct training data via queries
  • Adversarial examples — tiny input changes → big errors
  • Prompt injection — hidden instructions in input
  • Jailbreaking — social-engineer AI past guardrails

AI privacy: phrenology/physiognomy (unfounded trait inference), unprecedented-scale aggregation & identification, exacerbated secondary use & exclusion.

Bug Bounties & Vuln Disclosure

  • 3 entities: Bug hunters · Platforms (HackerOne) · Vendors
  • Failures: slow response, unclear scope, low payouts, legal risk
  • Full disclosure = publish immediately · Responsible = private report, 90-day embargo, then publish

Threat Modeling

  • CIA+: Confidentiality · Integrity · Availability · Authentication · Accountability
  • Risk = Asset × Threat × Vulnerability
  • Adversary (who) · Asset (target) · Threat (action) · Vulnerability (weakness)
  • Malware: Worm (self-propagating) · Virus (needs human action) · PUP (deceptive about purpose)

Key Case Studies

  • "Why Johnny Can't Encrypt" (1999): 12 CS students, only 3 sent encrypted email; 3 emailed private key. PGP UI too confusing.
  • Google Buzz: auto-enrolled Gmail users, exposed contacts publicly → FTC fine.
  • Snapchat: "disappearing" messages could persist → FTC action.
  • Hypocrite commits (2021): submitted malicious code to Linux kernel → violated beneficence & respect for persons.
  • Expert vs non-expert (Munyendo): experts prioritize updates+pw managers+2FA; non-experts rely on antivirus. Experts click more unknown links (assess risk). Non-experts overestimate antivirus.
